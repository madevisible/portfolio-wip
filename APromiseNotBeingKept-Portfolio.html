A Promise Not Being KeptAnalyst's Note
How this analysis came about

I came to this site as a user, looking for information the way people do when they are trying to understand something that matters to them. Nobody asked me to look closely. Nobody is paying me to have an opinion. And something kept not quite working, not in one obvious place, but as a feeling that accumulated: the site implied more than it delivered.

I notice that kind of friction. It is what I am trained to notice. So I started looking more carefully, and then more carefully still. One gap pointed to another. One outdated claim led me to the primary source, which led me to realise how far the site had drifted from what it was supposed to say. What began as a user's frustration became a structured question: does this site actually do what it says it does?

The answer took shape over several weeks of close reading, source verification, and the kind of thinking that happens when you keep asking why something feels wrong until you reach the structural reason underneath. This document is where that process ended up. It was produced entirely on my own initiative, without commission, without access to internal data, and without the organisation's knowledge.

It is not a criticism of the people involved. It is an argument about what happens when capacity runs out before the work is done, and about who bears the cost of that gap.

What I did: method and approach

01
Direct site review. A systematic, page-by-page examination of the live website, documenting every gap between what the site implies and what it actually delivers: broken links, missing content, outdated clinical criteria, inaccessible rights information.
02
Primary source verification. All findings cross-referenced against DSM-5, Statistics Iceland, the Icelandic Grænbók (2024), the OECD Iceland immigrant integration report (2024), peer-reviewed Nordic ADHD cost analyses, and Icelandic legal frameworks around disability rights and accommodation.
03
Structural diagnosis. Identifying the argument beneath the surface findings: these are not maintenance failures. They are the predictable result of a capacity problem, and that distinction matters, because it points toward a different kind of solution.
04
Synthesis into a usable argument. Translating the analysis into a format that makes the problem impossible to ignore and the solution concrete, costed, and actionable.

The core finding

The organisation has chosen to host diagnosis information, rights information, and system navigation guidance. The question is no longer whether it should provide this. It already has. The question is whether it provides it accurately, accessibly, and for everyone who needs it.

In its current state, the site does not meet that standard

That is not a neutral position. It is a promise that is not being kept. And the people it fails are precisely the people who came to the site because they needed help.

A Promise Not Being Kept1. The Scale of the Challenge

1. The scale of the challenge

Iceland has the highest rate of ADHD medication use in the Nordic countries. 6.3% of the total population, all ages and genders, are currently on stimulant medication.1 Behind that number are real people: adults who spent years not knowing why things were hard, parents trying to understand what their child is entitled to, people who finally have a name for what they experience, now trying to figure out what comes next.

A note on diagnosis rates

Iceland's high medication rate has attracted scrutiny. A study published in the Icelandic Medical Journal (2024) concluded that use is two to three times higher than internationally expected prevalence rates, prompting the Ministry of Health to establish a working group. This debate does not diminish the need for accurate, accessible information. If anything, it increases it. People navigating a contested landscape need reliable guidance more, not less.

The Directorate of Health estimates prevalence at 3.4–7.2% among youth and 2.5–6.8% among adults, which translates to between 9,800 and 36,000 adults who may be living with ADHD, a significant share of them undiagnosed or still waiting.1,6

During that wait: no formal diagnosis, no access to medication, no legal basis for workplace or school accommodations. Children fall behind without a documented reason. Adults manage jobs, relationships, and daily life without knowing what they are managing, or what help they are entitled to. What they need, now, while they wait, is reliable information. That information is supposed to live on the site I reviewed.

2. What is at stake: the cost of getting it wrong

Waiting has a cost. Without a diagnosis, none of the daily consequences of an unmanaged condition have a name. They are read by employers, teachers, partners, and the person themselves as a character flaw. Repeated failure to meet expectations that feel reasonable to everyone else produces not just practical difficulties but chronic self-blame and eroded confidence. What these people need, now, is accurate information about what they are entitled to.

A Promise Not Being Kept3. What a Diagnosis Unlocks

3. What a diagnosis actually unlocks

A diagnosis is not only a clinical label. In Iceland, it is the gateway to a set of concrete, legally grounded rights. Many people on the waiting list do not know exist, because the information is not clearly presented anywhere they can find it.

Medication costs

Covered under Sjúkratryggingar Íslands. Adults pay out of pocket up to 22,000 ISK/year, then 15%, then 7.5%. Annual personal maximum: ~62,000 ISK. Without this, many assume medication is simply unaffordable.15

Workplace rights

Iceland is bound by the UN CRPD (ratified 2016) and the EU Employment Equality Directive, both of which establish employer duty to provide reasonable accommodations for neuropsychological conditions including ADHD.16

Educational accommodations

University students with neuropsychological diagnoses are entitled to extended exam time, separate examination rooms, and note-taking support. Secondary schools are required by Icelandic law to provide support.17,18

None of this is currently accessible in usable form

The rights section contains eight pages of headings with no application processes, no agency contacts, and three broken links. The information that determines whether people can afford medication, keep a job, or stay in education exists on the site in name only.

None of the rights information is available in any language other than Icelandic. This matters more than it might appear.

English is not a foreign language in Iceland. It is the default shared language. Keeping the site Icelandic-only is no longer a neutral default. It is a structural decision about who the information serves. With 21% of residents having an immigrant background, and that proportion rising every year, the decision to remain Icelandic-only excludes a growing share of the population that already faces higher rates of late diagnosis and employment instability.

A Promise Not Being Kept5. Clinical Accuracy

5. Clinical information: frozen in 1994

Consider a newly diagnosed adult who comes to the site wanting to understand their diagnosis. They find a page stating that symptoms must have been present before age 7. They think: that was not me. Maybe they have it wrong.

That criterion has been out of date since 2013.

What the site currently says
Before age 7
DSM-IV criterion, published 1994
Current clinical standard
Before age 12
DSM-5, in force since 2013
Critical finding, verified by direct site inspection

The diagnosis page still uses the DSM-IV 'before age 7' criterion. DSM-5 has been the operative standard since 2013. Adults who developed symptoms between ages 7 and 12 may be incorrectly concluding, based on information on this site, that they do not qualify for diagnosis. The site also references population statistics from 2008. A person reading them today has no way of knowing they are not current.

These are not obscure edge cases. The DSM-5 age-of-onset change was one of the most significant updates in the revision, specifically designed to capture adults who were missed under the old criteria. Getting this wrong does not just misinform. It actively discourages people from seeking help they are entitled to.

A Promise Not Being Kept7. What This Shows & What Could Change

7. What this shows, and what could change

The gaps identified here are not technical. They are content problems: what the site says, how it is structured, and who it reaches. These are precisely the kinds of problems that content strategy, plain-language rewriting, information architecture, and a sustainable maintenance framework can fix.

The case for external grant funding is strong. The work is clearly scoped. The need is evidenced. The target population is defined and the gap is measurable. Nordic and Icelandic funding bodies, including Velferðarráðuneytið, patient organisation grants and Nordic health cooperation funds, routinely support exactly this kind of content and knowledge infrastructure project.

A one-year project role would deliver:

1
Full content audit. Every page and every claim documented, gaps prioritised, with a clear action plan
2
Rewritten core texts, ready to publish: diagnosis information aligned with DSM-5, rights overview, support pathways
3
English translation of key content, starting with the sections most critical for non-Icelandic-speaking families: rights, medication, accommodations
4
Costed technical recommendations, specific and structured, ready to hand to an external developer
5
Content maintenance framework: templates, update schedules and editorial guidelines so the organisation can sustain accuracy going forward

The knowledge is there. The data is there. The intent is there. What is missing is dedicated resource: someone whose only job is to take what exists and make it work for the people who need it.

This analysis demonstrates what that work looks like in practice: starting from a user's experience, following the evidence, identifying the structural cause beneath the surface symptom, and producing an argument that makes the problem impossible to ignore and the solution impossible to dismiss.
