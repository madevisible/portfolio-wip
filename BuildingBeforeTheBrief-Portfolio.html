The question

What users need from a technical services website

Users arriving at a technical services website typically have a specific, practical problem. They need to establish quickly whether the company can help them, what that involves, and how to make contact. If the site does not support that sequence directly, the consequence is often invisible internally: lower conversion, weaker trust signals, and increased abandonment, with no clear feedback mechanism to identify the cause.

The more common pattern is a site built to reflect internal priorities: service categories that map to how the company is organised, language that assumes industry familiarity, and calls to action that are understated. The result is content that functions well for people who already know the company, and less well for those who do not.

This case examines that pattern in a specific context: a website redesign for a mid-sized technical services company, initiated by a supplier offer and a recent acquisition, produced without prior alignment on user priorities, content ownership, or brand governance. The analysis identifies the structural conditions that limited the outcome, and what a more user-centred approach would have addressed.
01 — The Starting Point

Structural problems in the existing site

Corex Technical Services had operated for over 40 years. Their website had developed incrementally, with pages added as internal needs arose rather than as part of a structured content model. Several problems had accumulated by the time the redesign was initiated.

Undifferentiated audience addressing

Private customers, public institutions, and commercial contractors were addressed from the same homepage with the same entry point and the same language. No routing, no hierarchy, no differentiation by user need or context.

Fragmented and outdated content

Information was distributed across multiple pages, some several years old. The same service appeared in different forms in different sections, with no clear indication of which was current or authoritative.

Internally oriented language

Copy reflected how the company categorised its own services rather than how a user would search for or evaluate them. Terminology assumed familiarity with the industry and the company's internal structure.

Absent or weak calls to action

There was no clear conversion path. A user wanting to book a job had to navigate across multiple pages to establish who to contact and how, with no single prioritised action visible on arrival.

Generic visual content

The site relied on stock photography with no connection to actual staff, locations, or work. This is a common constraint, but it reduces the specificity and credibility of the content it accompanies.

No stated value proposition

The site described service categories but did not articulate what made the company a specific, considered choice for a specific user. The absence of a clear value proposition is particularly consequential on a homepage competing for a user's attention and trust.

02 — Project Initiation

How the redesign came about

The redesign was initiated by two overlapping circumstances. The company's web provider offered a no-cost redesign as part of a long-standing contract, and the company had recently acquired a smaller regional firm, Mikkelsen Drift & Montage, which had been experiencing financial difficulties common to smaller independent trades businesses. Both events occurred within the same period.

The formal arrangement for the acquisition was that the two companies would operate as equal partners on a shared digital platform, each retaining its own name, identity, and customer relationships. In practice, the two organisations differed significantly in scale, geographic base, and organisational culture. Mikkelsen Drift & Montage had a strong local identity and a customer base built on long-standing personal relationships. The question of how to represent that partnership on a shared website was more structurally complex than the formal arrangement acknowledged.

Key implication for the project: The redesign was triggered by an available resource and an integration requirement, not by a strategic assessment of what the site needed to do differently for its users. The project began without a shared brief, agreed user priorities, or a defined content strategy. These conditions shaped what the finished site could become.

The web provider's role is also relevant to the governance picture. The contract allowed the company to make minor updates independently, but structural changes required the provider's involvement. The provider acted on instructions received by email or phone, without a content strategy or governance document to reference. This made consistent, user-oriented decision-making across the site harder to maintain over time.

03 — Structural Conditions

Factors that shaped what was possible

Several conditions were in place before production began that determined the ceiling for what the redesign could achieve.

1

Distributed ownership across service specialisms

The company had multiple partners, each with primary responsibility for a different service area: private customers, public institutions, commercial projects. Content decisions touching those areas required input from the relevant partner. There was no single content authority. Content priorities were therefore negotiated rather than set against a shared user model.

2

No budget for original visual content

No photography was commissioned for the redesign. The acquired company's premises, staff, and work could not be documented. The web provider's design drafts arrived with placeholder stock images sourced from unrelated contexts, requiring every image to be reviewed and replaced manually. Stock imagery in a trades context signals generic rather than specific, directly affecting the credibility of the content around it.

3

Content work understood as text production

Within the organisation, content production was understood primarily as a writing task: draft text, supply images, hand to the provider. The research, structuring, and iterative revision involved in producing user-oriented content was not part of the shared understanding of the work. This affected how the project was resourced and how it was prioritised alongside other operational demands.

4

Unresolved brand relationship between the two companies

The formal equal-partner arrangement did not resolve the practical question of how the two companies should be presented together on a single platform. Questions about naming, visual hierarchy, and content allocation were addressed during production rather than before it. The unresolved relationship between the two brands was effectively pushed into the website structure and content decisions.

04 — Production

How the conditions affected the work

Content decisions were made incrementally throughout production, often in response to stakeholder input rather than against a defined user model. Content drafted with user needs as the primary frame was sometimes revised to reflect what was internally significant at a given moment: a recently acquired qualification in a specialist area, a service a particular partner wanted to emphasise, a distinction between the two companies that needed to be preserved. This is a common pattern in organisations where content strategy has not been established as a function. It reflects the absence of a shared reference point, not a lack of care for the user.

The dual-brand challenge in practice. Presenting Corex Technical Services and Mikkelsen Drift & Montage as equal partners on a single platform required every design element to satisfy two separate identities simultaneously. Without a prior agreement on how the partnership should be represented visually and structurally, these decisions were made case by case during production. The result reflected the negotiation process rather than a coherent user experience.

05 — Outcome

What was delivered

The site launched with substantially improved content: more consistent, more accurately written, and better structured than what it replaced. Navigation was simplified. The acquisition was integrated. The site was a functional improvement on the brief it was given.

Several structural questions remained unresolved throughout production, limiting how coherent the final user experience could become. The three-audience problem was not resolved at the information architecture level. The brand relationship between the two companies was represented but not clearly defined for the user. The absence of original photography meant the visual layer remained generic.

The production timeline extended significantly beyond initial expectations. In part this reflected the content complexity involved. In part it reflected the structural conditions: content decisions that touched on unresolved strategic questions required additional alignment before they could be finalised. In an organisation without an established content function, that alignment had to be built iteratively rather than drawn from an agreed brief.

06 — Redesign Proposal

Here is what I would have done differently

The most consequential difference between the project as it ran and a more effective version of it is not a design decision. It is prior alignment on four questions that need agreed answers before production begins.

Required before production: Who are the primary user types, and which takes priority on the homepage? What does each user type need to accomplish in their first interaction with the site? Who has decision-making authority on content questions? And how is the relationship between the two companies defined, specifically enough to be designed and maintained consistently?

With those questions answered, content and structural decisions can be evaluated against a shared reference point rather than negotiated individually. The three-audience problem has a straightforward structural solution once the priority order is agreed: audience-specific entry points from the homepage, with content and language calibrated to each user's context and task.

The dual-brand question also has a structural solution, but it requires a governance decision first. If Corex Technical Services is the primary company on the platform and Mikkelsen Drift & Montage is a partner with a distinct local identity, that relationship can be designed clearly: a single primary brand in the navigation, with Mikkelsen Drift & Montage given its own defined section rather than competing for space across every page. Both companies retain what differentiates them. The user has a coherent experience of each.

So, were they speaking to themselves?

In structural terms, yes. The site reflected how the organisation understood itself more than how a user would arrive at it. That is a common outcome when a redesign begins without agreed answers to the foundational questions: who is the site for, what does each user type need to do, and who has the authority to make those answers consistent across the content.

The organisational conditions described here are not unusual. Distributed ownership, limited visual content budgets, and unresolved brand relationships are common constraints in smaller organisations undertaking a web redesign without an established content function. What changes the outcome is whether those conditions are identified and addressed before production begins, or worked around after the fact.

The structural and language decisions in the proposal above are straightforward to execute once the governance questions are resolved. That prior conversation is where the work actually starts.
