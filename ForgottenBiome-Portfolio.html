The Forgotten Biome.Origin and Context
Where this came from

In the summer of 2024, I began writing a story for my children, something to make long car rides pass. It was supposed to be about elves, rainbows and gentle magic. But somewhere along the way, it grew into something else: a layered universe built around ecological systems, the slow erosion of connection, and the idea that what has been forgotten can be remembered rather than replaced.

The world of Fulla, with its missing bees, its mycelium networks tended by industrious worms, and its mole who listens to the earth, was not designed against a theoretical framework. It emerged intuitively, from the inside out. It was only later, when I began to research the application for CCP Games, that I recognised how precisely the world's internal logic maps onto documented thinking in ecological systems theory, narrative game design and transmedia world-building.

That alignment between intuitive construction and theoretical grounding is what this sample is about. Not the story itself, but what the story demonstrates about how I think.

The structural core

The world of Fulla is built around three interconnected systems that mirror real ecological logic: pollinators (the bees, who maintain rhythm and connection above ground), mycorrhizal networks (the mycelium, tended by the worms below ground), and decomposers (the compost worms, who transform what was into what can be). This three-layer structure was not borrowed from ecology. It arrived through the internal logic of the story. That it matches what Suzanne Simard documented in her landmark research on forest communication networks (Nature, 1997) is not a coincidence. It is what happens when systems thinking is applied to world-building with genuine attention.

Intuition and research landing in the same place

The world's core design principle: that connection erodes gradually, that the absence feels wrong before it can be named, and that restoration requires listening rather than action, maps directly onto Robin Wall Kimmerer's concept of the "grammar of animacy" (Braiding Sweetgrass, 2013) and Ursula K. Le Guin's "Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction" (1986): the idea that the most powerful stories are not about conquest but about gathering, tending and remembering. Neither source was consulted during the writing. The world arrived at the same conclusions by following its own internal logic.

The translation

When the CCP Games application appeared, the question was not whether to apply. It was whether the world of Fulla could survive translation into a radically different context without losing what made it coherent. The answer turned out to be yes, and the process of finding that answer is itself the demonstration.

Source world
The Realm of the Álfar

A sensation beneath reality. Held together by rhythm and connection. Missing bees. Fading colours. A silence that grows loud. One who notices before she understands, and another who chooses to listen.

→
Translated world
The Forgotten Biome

A world thought extinct at the edge of space. A signal older than the technology detecting it. A planet that healed itself, slowly, stubbornly. Players who restore resonance rather than extract resources.

The core logic survives intact across the translation: connection erodes gradually and without announcement; restoration requires attention and patience, not force; the system has its own memory and will find its rhythm again if someone creates the conditions. What changes is the container: the aesthetics, the mechanics, the audience. What does not change is the underlying design philosophy.

The design principle that drives both worlds

"The consequences wouldn't be moral in a binary sense. They would be rhythmic. Each player's decisions could alter the planet's tone, colour and atmospheric behaviour, quietly feeding back into the wider network. A living metaphor for how balance, once disturbed, must be felt before it can be fixed."

2. What each world contains

Álfar world

The Western Tree

Not a deity but a structure. The backbone of the ecological system. Everything orients around it not because it commands but because it endures. It is not to be saved, only remembered.

Forgotten Biome

The Flicker

A signal that should not exist. Older than the technology detecting it. Not a distress call. A resonance. The planet's memory, still trying to reconnect. Following it does not lead to conquest, but to understanding.

Álfar world

The Bees

Not insects but rhythm. They maintained connection through pattern and dance. They did not disappear with drama. They withdrew quietly, when the connections they served had already begun to break.

Forgotten Biome

The Old Woman

A passenger whose memory drifts between clarity and confusion. She recognises the signal before anyone else does. Not because she is special. Because she remembers. She draws symbols. Whispers names no one knows yet.

Álfar world

The Compost Worms

"They turn what was into what can be, not with spells, but with rhythm, teamwork, and time." The underground workers. The ones who do what must be done, without ceremony, because the system requires it.

Forgotten Biome

The Pilots

Assigned to protect the refugees, they begin to see something shift in themselves: patterns, reciprocity, a different kind of intelligence. They arrive as extractors. The world asks them to become stewards.

3. Where the thinking connects to documented research

The following connections were identified after the work was done, not before. They are offered here as evidence that the intuitive approach produces results consistent with established theory, not as a claim to academic credentials.

Simard (1997) Nature: forest communication networks

The three-layer ecological structure in Fullas world (pollinators above ground, mycelium networks below, decomposers transforming what was) directly mirrors Simard's documented model of forest interdependence. The worms explicitly install and maintain the mycelium network as their central function, arrived at through narrative logic, not ecological research.

Le Guin (1986) The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction

Le Guin argues that the dominant narrative form: the hero's journey, the act of conquest, represents only one way of telling stories, and not necessarily the most honest one. The alternative is the carrier bag: gathering, tending, remembering. Fulla is not a hero. She is someone who notices and insists. The world is not saved. It is reconnected.

Kimmerer (2013) Braiding Sweetgrass

Kimmerer's concept of the "grammar of animacy": treating the natural world as subject rather than object, as something with its own intelligence and intention, is the philosophical foundation of both worlds. The planet heals itself. The bees withdraw when the connections they served are broken. The earth has memory. These are not metaphors in these worlds. They are structural facts.

Mateas and Stern (2005) Façade: emergent narrative design

The Forgotten Biome's gameplay logic: consequences that are rhythmic rather than binary, a world that responds to attention and care rather than to force, is a practical application of emergent narrative design: systems that generate story through interaction rather than scripted outcomes. "Solving it doesn't lead to conquest or loot. It leads to understanding."

Jenkins (2006) Convergence Culture: transmedia storytelling

The same world exists as children's book chapters, a rolleplaying game concept, a ceremonial song in both Danish and Icelandic, and a sci-fi game pitch. This is not fragmentation. It is a world large enough to support multiple entry points without losing its core. Each version adds something the others cannot. The logic holds across all of them.

The Forgotten Biome.Excerpt and Method

4. From the concept sheet

The Forgotten Biome: narrative premise (excerpt)

Maybe it begins with a flicker. A signal that should not exist, faint, fractured, older than the technology detecting it.

A transport ship, running low on rations and carrying survivors from a world lost to its own greed, picks it up by chance. Most dismiss it as static. But one of the oldest passengers, a woman whose memory drifts between clarity and confusion, suddenly changes. Her eyes light up. She starts drawing symbols, whispering names no one recognises.

Tracing the flicker leads to a world thought extinct: a failed terraforming site long abandoned. But life has returned, in forms no one expected. The planet has healed itself, slowly, stubbornly, beautifully.

The mission changes. It is no longer about extraction or settlement, but restoration: helping the refugees rebuild on nature's terms, not their own. And for the pilots assigned to protect them, something begins to shift: they start to see patterns, balance, reciprocity, a different kind of intelligence, older than code, wiser than profit.

From the CCP Games application, 2025. Submitted to the candidate bank.

5. How it was made

01
Built from the inside out. The world of Fulla was not designed against a brief or a theory. It emerged through writing, for children, for a car ride, without an audience in mind. The ecological logic arrived because it felt structurally right, not because it was planned.
02
Tested against a real brief. When the CCP Games application appeared, the question was whether the world could survive translation into an entirely different context. The process of answering that question, finding what was structural and what was decorative, is itself the demonstration of narrative design thinking.
03
AI as research and iteration partner. ChatGPT was used for visual documentation of the world-building materials and to pressure-test the translation logic. Perplexity was used to identify the theoretical connections after the work was done. The creative decisions, the world's internal logic and the translation choices were mine throughout.
04
Recognised the pattern after the fact. The theoretical connections to Simard, Le Guin, Kimmerer and Jenkins were identified through research conducted after the world was built. That they exist is not a claim to academic knowledge. It is evidence that the intuitive approach produces structurally sound results.

6. What it shows

This sample demonstrates a specific kind of thinking: the ability to build worlds with internal ecological logic, to identify what is structural versus what is contextual in that logic, and to translate the structural core into a new context without losing coherence. It also demonstrates something less common: that this thinking operates intuitively, arriving at conclusions consistent with established theory without working from that theory directly.

The same world exists in four forms: a children's book, a roleplay concept for a group of friends' children, a ceremonial song in Danish and Icelandic, and a sci-fi game pitch for one of the world's most complex persistent universes. Each version is complete in itself. The logic holds across all of them.

This material was produced independently, first for personal reasons, then in response to a professional opportunity. The CCP Games application was submitted to their candidate bank in 2024. The theoretical connections documented here were identified after the work was done, through research conducted during the preparation of this portfolio sample.

It is offered as a demonstration of narrative systems thinking, ecological world-building and transmedia translation, and as evidence that intuitive creative work and analytical rigour are not opposites.
